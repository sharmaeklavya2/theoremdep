TheoremDep

Degree of product of polynomials

Dependencies:

  • Polynomial
  • Integral Domain

Let $p, q \in R[x]$. Then $\operatorname{deg}(pq) \le \operatorname{deg}(p) + \operatorname{deg}(q)$. If coefficients of $p$ and $q$ are from an integral domain, $\operatorname{deg}(pq) = \operatorname{deg}(p) + \operatorname{deg}(q)$.

Proof

$(pq)_i = \sum_{j=0}^i p_jq_{i-j} \in R$.

Let $k = \operatorname{deg}(p) + \operatorname{deg}(q)$. Let $i > k$

\begin{align} & j \le \operatorname{deg}(p) \\ &\Rightarrow i-j \ge k+1-j \\ &= \operatorname{deg}(p) + \operatorname{deg}(q) + 1 - j \\ &= \operatorname{deg}(q) + 1 + (\operatorname{deg}(p) - j) \\ &> \operatorname{deg}(q) \\ &\Rightarrow q_{i-j} = 0 \end{align}

So, if $j \le \operatorname{deg}(p)$, $q_{i-j} = 0$ and if $j > \operatorname{deg}(p)$, $p_j = 0$. Therefore, $p_jq_{i-j} = 0$ for all $0 \le j \le i$.

$(pq)_i = \sum_{j=0}^i p_jq_{i-j} = 0$ for all $i > k$. Therefore, $pq \in R[x]$ and $\operatorname{deg}(pq) \le \operatorname{deg}(p) + \operatorname{deg}(q)$.

\begin{align} & j < \operatorname{deg}(p) \\ &\Rightarrow k-j \\ &= \operatorname{deg}(q) + \operatorname{deg}(p) - j \\ &> \operatorname{deg}(q) \\ &\Rightarrow q_{k-j} = 0 \end{align}

\begin{align} & (pq)_k = \sum_{j=0}^{\operatorname{deg}(p)-1} p_jq_{k-j} + p_{\operatorname{deg}(p)}q_{k-\operatorname{deg}(p)} + \sum_{j=\operatorname{deg}(p)+1}^k p_jq_{k-j} \\ &= \sum_{j=0}^{\operatorname{deg}(p)-1} p_j0 + p_{\operatorname{deg}(p)}q_{\operatorname{deg}(q)} + \sum_{j=\operatorname{deg}(p)+1}^k 0q_{k-j} \\ &= p_{\operatorname{deg}(p)}q_{\operatorname{deg}(q)} \end{align}

By definition, $p_{\operatorname{deg}(p)} \neq 0$ and $q_{\operatorname{deg}(q)} \neq 0$. If $R$ is an integral domain, their product is non-zero. Therefore, $\operatorname{deg}(pq) = k = \operatorname{deg}(p) + \operatorname{deg}(q)$.
