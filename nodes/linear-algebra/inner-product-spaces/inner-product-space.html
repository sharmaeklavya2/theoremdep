Inner product space

Dependencies:

  • Vector Space

Let $F$ be a field such that $F$ contains 3 disjoint (but not necessarily exhaustive) subsets: $\{0\}, F^+, F^-$ such that:

  • $x \in F^+ \iff (-x) \in F^-$.
  • $x, y \in F^+ \Rightarrow (x + y \in F^+ \wedge xy \in F^+)$.

The conjugate is a function from $F$ to $F$. The conjugate of $x$ is denoted as $\overline{x}$. It satisfies these properties:

  • $\overline{xy} = \overline{x}\overline{y}$.
  • $\overline{x + y} = \overline{x} + \overline{y}$.
  • $\overline{\overline{x}} = x$.
  • $x \neq 0 \Rightarrow x\overline{x} \in F^+$. $x\overline{x}$ is also denoted as $|x|^2$.
  • $x \in F^+ \Rightarrow \overline{x} = x$.

\[ \overline{0} = \overline{0 + 0} = \overline{0} + \overline{0} \Rightarrow \overline{0} = 0 \] \[ 0 = \overline{0} = \overline{x+(-x)} = \overline{x} + \overline{(-x)} \Rightarrow \overline{(-x)} = -\overline{x} \] \[ \overline{1} = 0 \Rightarrow 1 = \overline{\overline{1}} = \overline{0} = 0 \Rightarrow \bot \Rightarrow \overline{1} \neq 0 \Rightarrow \overline{1}^{-1} \textrm{ exists} \] \[ \overline{1} = \overline{1 \cdot 1} = \overline{1} \cdot \overline{1} \Rightarrow \overline{1} = 1 \]

Let $V$ be a vector space over $F$.

The inner product is a function from $V \times V$ to $F$. The inner product of $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ is denoted as $\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle$.

$V$ is an inner product space if it satisfies the following properties:

  • Conjugate symmetry: $\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = \overline{\langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u} \rangle}$.
  • Linearity in the first argument:
    • $\langle \mathbf{u_1} + \mathbf{u_2}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = \langle \mathbf{u_1}, \mathbf{v} \rangle + \langle \mathbf{u_2}, \mathbf{v} \rangle$.
    • $a\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = \langle au, \mathbf{v} \rangle$.
  • Positive definiteness:
    • (positive semidefiniteness) $\langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle \in F^+ \cup \{0\}$.
    • $\langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = 0 \iff \mathbf{v} = 0$.

$\langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle$ is also denoted as $\|\mathbf{v}\|^2$.
